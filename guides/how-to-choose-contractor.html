How to Choose a Contractor Without Getting Burned

What to verify before hiring any roofing, siding, gutter, or exterior contractor — from licensing and insurance to spotting scammers before they cost you thousands.

By Dustin Stohler, JD · May 23, 2026

← All Articles

Hiring the wrong contractor is one of the most expensive mistakes a homeowner can make. Storm chasers disappear after they cash your check. Uninsured workers get injured on your property and you bear the liability. Uncertified installers void your manufacturer warranty before the first year is up. I spent five years running a roofing company and another decade in law and HR before building Otter Quotes — and this guide covers everything I wish every homeowner knew before signing a contract.

1

Verify Licensing — Every State Has Different Requirements

Contractor licensing requirements vary significantly from state to state. Some states require a state-level license for all roofing work. Others delegate licensing to the county or city level. A small number of states have minimal licensing requirements. The only way to know whether your contractor is properly licensed for your location is to look it up yourself.

This matters because a license indicates minimum competency requirements, financial responsibility thresholds, and regulatory oversight. An unlicensed contractor is one whose background and qualifications no one has independently reviewed against your state's standards.

  • Ask for the contractor's license number before signing anything. Any legitimate contractor will provide it without hesitation.
  • Look it up on your state's licensing board website. Search "[your state] contractor license lookup" to find the official verification portal. Check that the license is active, not expired or suspended.
  • Confirm the license covers the work being performed. Some states have separate licenses for roofing, general contracting, and specialty trades. A general contractor license does not automatically authorize roofing work in all jurisdictions.
  • Check for any disciplinary actions on the license record. Many state licensing boards publish complaint history and disciplinary actions publicly.
Important: A contractor who offers to give you a discount if you "pay cash and skip the paperwork" is asking you to share the risk of an unlicensed job. Any work-related injury, structural defect, or dispute becomes significantly harder to resolve when there is no permit trail.

Confirm Insurance Coverage: Commercial General Liability and Workers' Compensation

Contractor insurance protects you. Without it, you may be personally liable for injuries that occur on your property, and there may be no recourse when work is defective and the contractor has no assets to pursue.

There are two critical insurance types to verify:

  • Commercial General Liability (CGL): This covers property damage and bodily injury that occurs as a result of the contractor's work. If a contractor drops equipment that damages your gutters or a subcontractor's ladder punches a hole in your siding, CGL pays for it. Ask for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) and confirm the policy is active and the coverage limits are adequate for your project.
  • Workers' Compensation: This covers medical expenses and lost wages for workers injured on the job. In most states, employers above a minimum employee threshold are required to carry it. If a roofing crew member falls from your roof and the contractor has no workers' comp, your homeowner's insurance and personal assets can be exposed to a lawsuit. Sole proprietors may be legally exempt from the requirement — ask directly whether they carry it voluntarily.
What to request: Ask the contractor for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) naming you as the certificate holder. This allows you to verify coverage dates and confirm the policy hasn't lapsed. The certificate should be dated within the last 30 days.

Never accept a verbal assurance that insurance is "in place." Policies lapse. Contractors sometimes carry insurance long enough to win the job, then let it expire. Requesting the certificate costs you nothing and takes the contractor two minutes to produce from their insurer.

Why Surety Bonds Matter More Than Most Homeowners Realize

A surety bond is a three-party agreement between the contractor (the principal), a bonding company (the surety), and you (the obligee). If the contractor fails to complete the work, abandons the project, or causes damage they refuse to cover, you can make a claim against the bond.

Unlike insurance — which is designed to cover accidental damage — bonds are designed to cover the contractor's failure to perform. They are a separate layer of financial protection.

  • Check the bond amount: Bonds are not all equal. A $10,000 bond on a $40,000 roofing project leaves you underprotected. The bond amount should be proportional to the scope of work.
  • Verify the bond is active: Like insurance, bonds can lapse. Ask the contractor for the bond number and the bonding company's name, then call the bonding company to confirm the bond is active for the policy period covering your project.
  • Understand the claim process: Making a bond claim is not automatic. You typically need to document the contractor's default, give proper notice, and file within a specific time window. Understanding this before signing a contract puts you in a stronger position if something goes wrong.

Bonded contractors have passed a financial background review conducted by the surety. This is not a guarantee of quality, but it is an additional filter that screens out the most financially irresponsible operators.

Manufacturer Certifications — What They Mean for Your Warranty

Most major roofing and siding manufacturers offer tiered certification programs for contractors. These programs require contractors to complete product training, demonstrate installation volume, carry specific insurance levels, and in some cases submit to quality audits. In return, certified contractors can offer extended manufacturer warranties that uncertified installers simply cannot provide.

Here is why this matters for you: the standard manufacturer warranty on most roofing products is 25 to 50 years on materials. But if the product is installed by a contractor who is not certified by the manufacturer, many warranties are reduced or voided outright. A contractor who installs a $15,000 GAF roof without GAF certification may deliver you a 10-year limited warranty where a certified contractor would have delivered a 50-year system warranty.

Major manufacturers and their certification tiers (most offer two or three levels):

  • GAF: Certified Contractor, Master Elite (top tier, roughly 3% of roofers). Master Elite contractors can offer GAF's WindProven and Golden Pledge warranties — the strongest coverage available in residential roofing.
  • Owens Corning: Preferred Contractor, Preferred Platinum Contractor. Platinum-level contractors can offer the Total Protection Roofing System warranty.
  • CertainTeed: ShingleMaster, SELECT ShingleMaster (top tier). Top-tier contractors can offer the 5-Star Sure Start Plus warranty.
  • Atlas Roofing: Signature Select Contractor. Required for Atlas's Signature Select warranty.
  • IKO, Malarkey, PABCO, Tamko: Each operates its own certification program with corresponding warranty upgrades.
Ask the right question: "Are you certified by the manufacturer of the materials you're recommending, and what warranty tier does your certification qualify me for?" A contractor who can't answer this clearly either isn't certified or doesn't understand the warranty implications.

Red-Flag Tactics: How to Spot a Storm Chaser or Scammer

The roofing industry attracts a specific predatory contractor type known as a "storm chaser" — an operation that follows severe weather events, solicits homeowners aggressively in the days following a storm, collects deposits, and in many cases disappears or delivers substandard work before the insurance cycle closes. Recognizing their tactics protects you.

The following are the most reliable warning signs:

  • Unsolicited door-to-door solicitation after a storm. Legitimate local contractors don't need to knock on doors. They get referrals. A crew that appears in your neighborhood the day after a hailstorm and knocks on twenty doors in an hour is almost certainly a storm-chasing operation.
  • Claims to be an "insurance specialist" or "insurance claims expert." Contractors are licensed to repair property. They are not licensed to adjust insurance claims, interpret your policy, or negotiate with your insurer on your behalf. Any contractor who positions themselves as your insurance advocate is either misrepresenting their credentials or incentivized to inflate your claim — which exposes you to fraud liability.
  • Pressure to sign immediately. "This price is only good today" or "we have crews finishing nearby and can start tomorrow if you sign right now" are high-pressure tactics designed to prevent you from doing basic due diligence. Take the time to verify credentials.
  • Large upfront deposits, especially in cash. A reasonable deposit to secure materials is normal, typically 10 to 30 percent. Requests for 50 percent or more upfront, or for cash payment, are warning signs. Cash payments eliminate your dispute rights and can indicate unlicensed operations trying to avoid audit trails.
  • No local address or verifiable business presence. Search the company name and address. Check how long the business has existed. A contractor with no Google Business listing, no Better Business Bureau profile, and an address that returns no results is a significant risk.
  • Offers to waive your insurance deductible. This is illegal in most states and constitutes insurance fraud. A contractor who offers to "work around" your deductible is asking you to participate in fraud, regardless of how the offer is framed.
If you've already signed with a storm chaser: Review your contract's cancellation clause. Most states have a statutory right to cancel a home solicitation contract within three business days. Document everything and act quickly.

What Online Reviews Actually Tell You — and Their Limits

Online reviews are a valuable signal, but they require careful interpretation. Here is how to use them effectively.

Where to look:

  • Google Business Profile: Volume matters as much as rating. A contractor with 200 reviews averaging 4.6 stars is more credible than one with 8 reviews averaging 5.0. Look at how recent the reviews are — a sustained flow of recent reviews indicates an active operation.
  • Better Business Bureau (bbb.org): Look at the complaint count, the nature of the complaints (not showing up, poor workmanship, billing disputes), and critically, how the contractor responded. Companies that resolve BBB complaints demonstrate accountability. Companies that ignore them or have BBB accreditation revoked are higher risk.
  • Angi and HomeAdvisor: These platforms verify that reviewers actually hired the contractor, which reduces the fake-review problem. They also display background check status and licensing verification independently. Note that contractors pay for placement on these platforms, which does create a selection effect.

What reviews can't tell you:

  • Current insurance status. A contractor with great reviews last year may have let their insurance lapse. Always verify the COI directly.
  • Quality of the specific crew assigned to your job. Large contracting companies often subcontract work. The crew that arrives may not be the same people who earned those reviews.
  • Whether the work will hold up long-term. Most negative reviews about installation quality appear one to three years post-installation, when weather reveals the defects. A new company may have uniformly positive reviews simply because they haven't been in business long enough for problems to surface.
One-star reviews are often more informative than five-star reviews. Read how the contractor responded to criticism. A defensive or dismissive response to a documented complaint tells you more about how they'll handle a problem on your job than any positive review does.

How to Verify a Contractor Independently

Beyond asking the contractor directly, you have multiple channels to independently verify their credentials. This takes fifteen to thirty minutes and can prevent a five-figure mistake.

Independent Verification Checklist

  • Search your state's contractor licensing board website for the license number the contractor provided. Confirm it is active and matches the business name.
  • Call the insurance company listed on the Certificate of Insurance (not the contractor — the insurer) to confirm the policy is active.
  • Search the contractor's business name on your state's Secretary of State business entity database. Confirm the business is registered and in good standing.
  • Check the Better Business Bureau listing for complaint history and accreditation status.
  • Search the contractor's name and city in Google. Look for news articles, forum posts, or Nextdoor discussions related to complaints.
  • Verify manufacturer certifications directly on the manufacturer's website (GAF, Owens Corning, and CertainTeed all offer contractor lookup tools).
  • Request three references from jobs completed in the last twelve months. Call at least one. Ask about worksite cleanliness, communication, and whether they would hire the contractor again.

This may feel like a lot of work. It is — and that's exactly why many homeowners skip it and end up in disputes. The thirty minutes you invest before signing a contract is worth far more than the months of effort required to recover from a bad one.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a licensed and a bonded contractor?

Licensing and bonding are separate protections. Licensing indicates that a contractor has met your state or local government's minimum competency and regulatory requirements. A surety bond is a financial guarantee that protects you if the contractor fails to complete the work or causes damage they refuse to cover. A contractor can be licensed but not bonded, bonded but operating without a required license, or both. You should verify both independently.

Do I really need to call the insurance company to verify coverage?

For large projects — anything over $5,000 — calling to verify is worth the two minutes it takes. Certificates of Insurance can be altered, and policies do lapse. The insurer can confirm within seconds whether the policy number on the certificate is currently active. Ask to speak with the certificate of insurance department or commercial lines team.

How do I verify a manufacturer certification?

Each major manufacturer maintains a contractor lookup tool on their website. For GAF, search "find a GAF contractor" and look up by company name or zip code. Owens Corning and CertainTeed have similar tools. The lookup confirms both whether the contractor is certified and at what tier. If the contractor claims a certification that doesn't appear in the lookup, ask them to show you their certification documents directly from the manufacturer.

What should a legitimate contractor contract include?

A legitimate contractor agreement should include: a detailed scope of work describing exactly what materials will be used (manufacturer, product line, color), a payment schedule tied to project milestones rather than a calendar, the total contract price with any contingency provisions clearly described, the project start date and estimated completion timeline, warranty terms for both materials and workmanship, a process for handling change orders, and a cancellation clause. Anything that lacks a written scope of work or has payments front-loaded before work begins is a risk.

Is it safe to hire a contractor who subcontracts the work?

Subcontracting is common and not inherently problematic — most large roofing projects involve subcontractors for specialized trades. What matters is whether the general contractor maintains supervision, ensures their subcontractors carry appropriate insurance, and takes responsibility for the quality of the finished work under the contract. Ask directly: "Will your own crew be performing the work, or will you be subcontracting any portion of it?" If subcontractors are involved, ask how the general contractor supervises their work and whether the subcontractors are covered under the general contractor's insurance policy or carry their own.

What happens if my contractor damages my property during the job?

If the contractor carries Commercial General Liability insurance, property damage caused by their work is covered. Report the damage to the contractor in writing (email creates a timestamp and paper trail) immediately. If the contractor is unresponsive, you can file a claim directly with their insurer using the contact information on the Certificate of Insurance. This is one of the primary reasons verifying active CGL coverage before work begins is so important.
